This project implements a Naïve Bayes machine learning model to predict the likelihood of heart disease, using a dataset from the UCI Machine Learning Repository. It utilized CategoricalNB for categorical features and Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) for continuous data, combining both models to enhance prediction accuracy. The goal was to optimize both computational efficiency and classification performance while addressing the challenges of working with real-world healthcare data in an undergraduate collaborative setting. The results demonstrated the effectiveness of the hybrid approach, leveraging the strengths of Naïve Bayes for handling rare categories and KDE for continuous features, resulting in a robust and practical tool for heart disease prediction.

This research explores cellular automata (CA), algorithmic structures characterized by simple yet complex patterns. Each cell in a CA transitions through states based on a defined alphabet and neighborhood rules, evolving over discrete time steps. Using PascGaloisJE software, I modeled multidimensional CAs, focusing on analyzing fractal-dimensioned patterns. The study quantifies complexity by counting states and calculating the fractal dimension, particularly in sub-triangles, to understand the compositional and fractal properties of CA patterns. Through computational simulations, I aimed to uncover the intricate structure of CA-derived patterns.
