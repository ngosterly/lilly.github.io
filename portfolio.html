This project implemented a Naïve Bayes machine learning model to predict the likelihood of heart disease, using a dataset from the UCI Machine Learning Repository. It utilized CategoricalNB for categorical features and Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) for continuous data, combining both models to enhance prediction accuracy. The goal was to optimize both computational efficiency and classification performance while addressing the challenges of working with real-world healthcare data in a collaborative setting. The results demonstrated the effectiveness of the hybrid approach, leveraging the strengths of Naïve Bayes for handling rare categories and KDE for continuous features, resulting in a robust and practical tool for heart disease prediction.

Predicting Heart Disease Probability Using Naïve Bayes ML

This research explores cellular automata (CA), algorithmic structures characterized by simple yet complex patterns. Each cell in a CA transitions through states based on a defined alphabet and neighborhood rules, evolving over discrete time steps. Using PascGaloisJE software, I modeled multidimensional CAs, focusing on analyzing fractal-dimensioned patterns. The study quantifies complexity by counting states and calculating the fractal dimension, particularly in sub-triangles, to understand the compositional and fractal properties of CA patterns. Through computational simulations, I aimed to uncover the intricate structure of CA-derived patterns.

Investigations of Fractal Dimensions in Cellular Automata

This group research focused on exploring space-time diagrams of infinite one-dimensional cellular automata generated using modular alphabets and two-seed (varied) initial states. The study investigated how changing initial states affects the distribution and density of identity and non-identity elements within these diagrams. Additionally, it examined how the growth rate dimension, a measure of self-similar growth, varies with different parameters in cellular automata using alphabets defined by powers of primes. The overall goal was to understand the structural patterns and fractal-like behavior in these systems. /p>

This study examined gender biases in pronoun resolution by three popular Large Language Models (LLMs) — ChatGPT-3.5, ChatGPT-4.0, and Google's Gemini — by analyzing their default and alternative interpretations of ambiguous pronouns in sentences featuring various occupations and activities. Using various sentence prompts with multiple permutations (prompt engineering), the research evaluated the models' tendencies to associate specific pronouns with certain roles, revealing potential biases. The findings highlighted the ethical challenges of AI systems and conveyed the importance of equitable and accountable development to address gender bias in machine learning.

Hidden Gender Bias in Large-Language Models

This literature study examined the potential of Artificial Intelligence (AI) to address work-related fatigue in the healthcare industry, an issue exemplified by the pandemic and linked to increased errors, patient dissatisfaction, and high turnover rates. Drawing insights from AI's transformative applications in sectors like transportation and education, the study explored how AI can be integrated into healthcare to monitor staff stress levels, predict burnout, and enhance diagnostic accuracy for improved patient outcomes. It emphasized the importance of secure implementation, highlighting ethical considerations, data privacy, accountability, and adherence to regulatory frameworks as critical factors for success.

AI to Reduce Work-Related Fatigue in Healthcare
